SIM Swap Fraud in Nigeria: How It Works and How to Stop It

Criminals are calling your network provider right now, pretending to be you. Within minutes, your phone number is theirs — and so are the OTPs going to your bank account. Here is exactly how it works and five concrete steps to stop it.

📅 Published: March 2026 🕐 10 minute read ✍️ Chukwuemeka Oko, CyberWatch NG
⚠ Active Threat Alert

SIM swap fraud cases are rising sharply across Nigerian banks in 2026

GTBank, Access Bank, and First Bank customers have been among the most reported targets. If you have not set a SIM lock PIN on your network, you are currently at risk.

What Is SIM Swap Fraud?

Your phone number is the key to almost everything financial in Nigeria. When you want to approve a bank transfer, reset your mobile banking password, or confirm a large transaction, your bank sends an OTP (One-Time Password) to your phone number. This is meant to keep you safe.

SIM swap fraud exploits this system. A criminal convinces your network provider — MTN, Airtel, Glo, or 9mobile — to transfer your phone number to a new SIM card that they control. Once they have your number, every OTP your bank sends goes directly to them, not to you.

By the time you notice your phone has lost signal, your account may already be emptied.

Step-by-Step: How Criminals Do It

Understanding the exact process is the first step to protecting yourself. Here is how a typical SIM swap attack unfolds in Nigeria.

Step 1

They gather your personal information

Before anything else, the criminal collects your full name, phone number, date of birth, and sometimes your BVN. This information is often found on social media, bought from data brokers, or obtained through earlier phishing attacks.

Step 2

They call your network provider

Using your details, the criminal calls MTN, Airtel, Glo, or 9mobile customer service and claims they lost their SIM card or phone. They use your personal details to pass the identity verification questions.

Step 3

They get your number transferred

The network provider — believing they are speaking to you — transfers your phone number to a new SIM card in the criminal's possession. This process can take as little as 30 minutes.

Step 4

Your phone loses signal

Your existing SIM becomes deactivated. You notice your phone shows “No Service” or “Emergency Calls Only.” Many people assume it's a network issue and wait — which gives the criminal time to act.

Step 5 — The Attack

They drain your bank account

With your number, they reset your mobile banking password, receive OTPs, and complete transfers. They typically move money in multiple quick transactions to different accounts before you realise what is happening.

“I woke up to ₦1.8 million missing. My phone had no service since the night before. I thought it was MTN network problems.” — Victim testimony shared on Nairaland, January 2026 (name withheld)

Warning Signs You May Be a Target

Watch out for these signals that a SIM swap attack may already be in progress:

  • Sudden loss of phone signal — especially overnight or in an area where you normally have coverage
  • Unable to make or receive calls or texts even after restarting your phone
  • Unexpected password reset emails from your bank or email provider
  • Bank alerts for transactions you didn't make — if you still have data or WiFi access
  • Strange calls from people claiming you contacted them — criminals sometimes probe targets first
⚠ If your phone loses signal unexpectedly, do not wait. Immediately connect to WiFi and contact your bank and network provider using an alternative phone.

How to Protect Yourself: 5 Steps to Take Today

✓ Protection Step 1

Set a SIM lock PIN with your network provider

This is a secret PIN that must be provided before any changes — including SIM swaps — can be made to your account. Call your provider or visit a service centre to set this up today.

This is your single most effective protection against SIM swap fraud.
✓ Protection Step 2

Enable transaction notifications on a secondary email

Make sure your bank can reach you even when your phone is compromised. Set up email alerts for every transaction so suspicious activity is flagged regardless of your phone status.

✓ Protection Step 3

Use an authenticator app instead of SMS OTPs

Apps like Google Authenticator or Microsoft Authenticator generate OTP codes on your device itself — not via your SIM. Some Nigerian banks including GTBank and Access Bank now support this for mobile banking.

✓ Protection Step 4

Limit what personal information you share publicly

Your full name, date of birth, and phone number together are often enough to pass network provider verification. Review your Facebook, Instagram, and LinkedIn privacy settings today.

✓ Protection Step 5

Act immediately if your phone loses signal

Use WiFi to call your bank's fraud line and freeze your account. Then call your network provider from another phone. Every minute counts.

How to Set a SIM Lock PIN: By Network Provider

Network How to Set SIM Lock Method
MTN Dial *662# and follow the prompts to set your SIM PIN, or visit any MTN service centre USSD
Airtel Visit an Airtel service centre with valid ID and request a SIM swap restriction / SIM lock IN-PERSON
Glo Call Glo customer care on 121 and request a SIM lock PIN be added to your account CALL
9mobile Call 200 or visit a 9mobile experience centre to activate SIM protection CALL

💡 Pro tip: When visiting a service centre, bring your National ID, voter's card, or international passport. The more secure your account verification, the harder it is for criminals to bypass it.

What to Do If You've Already Been Targeted

In the first 30 minutes:

Use any available WiFi connection to log into your mobile banking app immediately and freeze or temporarily disable your account. Most Nigerian banking apps — GTBank, Access, Zenith, UBA — have a “freeze account” or “block card” option in settings. Do this before calling anyone.

Then contact your bank directly:

Call your bank's fraud line from a different phone. Have your account number, BVN, and recent transaction history ready. Report the incident and request a transaction reversal if funds have already been moved — some banks can reverse recent fraudulent transfers.

Report to the relevant authorities:

  • Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) — ncc.gov.ng
  • Economic and Financial Crimes Commission (EFCC) — efcc.gov.ng
  • ngCERT (Nigerian Computer Emergency Response Team) — cert.gov.ng
Protect someone you know

Share this article with a family member or colleague. One person warned is one account saved.

WhatsApp Hijacking in Nigeria: How Scammers Steal Accounts and How to Stop Them

One message. One 6-digit code. That is all it takes for a scammer to take over your WhatsApp, message your contacts, and beg for money in your name. Here is everything you need to know.

📅 Published: March 2026 🕐 8 minute read ✍️ Chukwuemeka Oko, CyberWatch NG
⚠ Why This Matters

WhatsApp is the most used app in Nigeria

With over 90 million Nigerian users, WhatsApp is where Nigerians do business, send money, and stay in touch with family. That makes it the #1 target for scammers. Every week, thousands of Nigerian accounts are hijacked using this exact method.

What Is WhatsApp Account Hijacking?

WhatsApp account hijacking is when a criminal takes full control of your WhatsApp account by tricking you into handing them your 6-digit registration code. Once they have that code, they can log into your account from their own phone — locking you out completely.

They then impersonate you to all your contacts — your family, friends, and colleagues — sending urgent money requests, spreading scam links, or using your account to hijack even more people.

The most dangerous part: it looks like it is coming from you.

How the Scam Works: Step by Step

Step 1

You receive a message from a “friend”

The message often comes from someone in your contacts whose WhatsApp has already been hijacked. It reads something like: “Hi, sorry to bother you. WhatsApp accidentally sent a 6-digit verification code to your number instead of mine. Can you send it to me? I need it urgently.”

Because the message comes from a familiar contact, you trust it.

Step 2

WhatsApp sends you a real code

While the scammer is chatting with you, they are simultaneously trying to log into your WhatsApp account on their device. WhatsApp sends a 6-digit SMS code to your phone number — the real registration code for your account — as part of their login attempt.

Step 3 — The Moment It Goes Wrong

You forward the code

Believing you are helping a friend, you send them the 6-digit code you just received. The moment you do, the scammer enters it on their device and takes complete control of your WhatsApp account. You are immediately logged out.

✗ That code was never theirs. It was yours. You just handed them the key to your account.
Step 4

They immediately use your account to scam your contacts

Within minutes, the criminal sends messages to everyone in your contact list. Common messages include:

  • “Please I need urgent help. I'm in trouble. Can you send me ₦20,000? I'll pay back tomorrow.”
  • “I just invested in this platform and made ₦150,000 in 3 days. Join here: [link]”
  • “I accidentally sent my WhatsApp code to your number. Please forward it to me.” — targeting your contacts with the same trick
“My mother sent ₦50,000 to a scammer because the message came from my number. She had no reason to doubt it was me.” — Reader account shared with CyberWatch NG, February 2026 (name withheld)

Other Ways Scammers Hijack WhatsApp Accounts

The code-forwarding trick is the most common, but it is not the only method. Be aware of these variations:

  • Voicemail interception: If your voicemail PIN is the default (0000 or 1234), scammers can call your number, let it go to voicemail, and listen to the 6-digit code WhatsApp reads out automatically. Change your voicemail PIN today.
  • Phishing links: A message urges you to click a link and “verify your WhatsApp account.” The fake page steals your login credentials.
  • SIM swap + WhatsApp takeover: As covered in our SIM Swap article, once a criminal controls your phone number they also control your WhatsApp, because WhatsApp uses your number for verification.

How to Protect Your WhatsApp Account: Do These Now

✓ Most Important: Enable Two-Step Verification

Add a 6-digit PIN to your WhatsApp account

Go to WhatsApp → Settings → Account → Two-step verification → Enable.

You will create a 6-digit PIN. From now on, even if someone gets your SMS verification code, they cannot access your account without this PIN. This is the single most effective protection available.

Do this right now before you finish reading this article. It takes 60 seconds.
✓ Protection Step 2

Never share any 6-digit code with anyone

WhatsApp will never ask you to forward a code to someone else. No legitimate person or business needs your WhatsApp verification code. If anyone asks for it — regardless of who they appear to be — it is a scam. Full stop.

✓ Protection Step 3

Change your voicemail PIN

Call your network provider or use their app to change your voicemail PIN from the default (0000 or 1234) to something unique. For MTN users, dial *123# to access account options. For Airtel, dial *432#.

✓ Protection Step 4

Verify before you trust — even with friends

If you receive an unusual request from a friend on WhatsApp — especially for money or a code — call them directly on their phone number to confirm. Do not reply on WhatsApp itself, as their account may already be compromised.

What to Do If Your WhatsApp Account Has Been Hijacked

Step 1: Try to log back in immediately

Open WhatsApp and enter your phone number. WhatsApp will send a new 6-digit SMS code to your number. Enter it. If you have Two-Step Verification enabled, you will also need your PIN. This will log the scammer out of your account.

Step 2: If that fails, contact WhatsApp support

Email support@whatsapp.com with the subject line “Lost/Stolen Account” and explain that your account was hijacked. Include your phone number in international format (e.g. +2348012345678). WhatsApp can deactivate the account to stop further damage.

Step 3: Warn your contacts immediately

Call or text your most important contacts using SMS or another platform and warn them that your WhatsApp was compromised. Tell them to ignore any messages or money requests sent from your account in the past few hours.

Step 4: Check what else may be at risk

If the scammer had access to your WhatsApp, check whether any linked accounts — especially WhatsApp Business or WhatsApp Pay — show suspicious activity. Also check if they accessed any documents or bank details you may have shared in chats.

Quick Summary: Your WhatsApp Protection Checklist

  • ✓ Enable Two-Step Verification: WhatsApp → Settings → Account → Two-step verification
  • ✓ Never share any 6-digit code with anyone, for any reason
  • ✓ Change your voicemail PIN from the default
  • ✓ Call to verify any unusual request — even from a known contact
  • ✓ If hijacked: re-register immediately, email WhatsApp support, warn your contacts
Share this with someone who needs it

Forward this to a parent, sibling, or colleague. WhatsApp hijacking spreads through contacts — breaking the chain starts with you.

Android Malware Alert: ngCERT Warns Nigerians About Phones Being Hacked Right Now

Nigeria’s official cybersecurity team has issued a formal warning that multiple dangerous malware families are actively spreading across Android phones in Nigeria right now. Your banking details, OTP codes, and personal data could already be at risk.

📅 Published: March 2026 🕐 8 minute read ✍️ Chukwuemeka Oko, CyberWatch NG
⚠ Active Threat — ngCERT Official Advisory, March 2026

This warning affects all Nigerians using Android smartphones

Over 182 million phone subscriptions are at risk. These are confirmed infections spreading across Nigerian networks and devices today — not theoretical threats.

What Is Happening?

ngCERT — the Nigerian Computer Emergency Response Team — has detected six malware families currently active in Nigeria’s digital space. These are confirmed infections spreading across Nigerian networks and devices today.

The Six Malware Families Identified

Malware 1

Android Backdoor

Opens a hidden access point into your phone for attackers. Lets criminals enter and operate on your device without your knowledge.

Malware 2

Triada

Embeds itself deep in your phone’s system. Extremely dangerous because it can survive a factory reset — meaning wiping your phone does not remove it.

Malware 3

HummingBad (Hummer)

Roots your device and installs additional malicious apps without your knowledge or consent, often silently in the background.

Malware 4

BADBOX / Prizmes

Linked to pre-installed malware found on budget Android phones sold in markets like Alaba and Computer Village. You are infected before you even use the phone.

Malware 5

Rootnik

Exploits vulnerabilities in your Android system to gain full administrative control of your device remotely.

Malware 6

Uupay

Specifically targets payment and banking apps. Designed to steal your financial login credentials and intercept transactions.

What Can This Malware Do to Your Phone?

  • Steal your mobile banking username and password
  • Intercept your OTP (One-Time Password) codes before they reach you
  • Read your private WhatsApp and SMS messages
  • Take full remote control of your phone
  • Add your phone to a criminal botnet used to attack other people
  • Remain active even after a factory reset (Triada variant)

How Are Nigerians Getting Infected?

Infection Route 1

Pre-installed on cheap phones

Some budget Android phones bought from Alaba market, Computer Village, and informal markets come with malware already installed in the firmware. You are infected before you even use the phone.

Infection Route 2

Repackaged apps from unofficial sources

Nigerians downloading apps from APK sites, Telegram channels, or WhatsApp forwards instead of the official Google Play Store. The fake app looks real but contains hidden malware.

Infection Route 3

Phishing links on WhatsApp and email

A message arrives with a link to “claim your prize,” “verify your BVN,” or “watch this video.” Clicking it installs the malware silently in the background.

How to Protect Yourself — Do These Right Now

✓ Step 1

Update your Android immediately

Go to Settings → About Phone → Software Update → Check for Updates. Security patches close the holes malware uses to get in. Do this today.

✓ Step 2

Only download apps from Google Play Store

Delete any app you downloaded from a website, WhatsApp forward, or Telegram channel. If it is not in the official Google Play Store, it is not safe.

✓ Step 3

Never click links in unsolicited messages

Not from unknown numbers. Not even from contacts — their phone may already be infected. Verify any unusual link by calling the sender directly on their number.

✓ Step 4

Watch for unusual phone behaviour

Battery draining unusually fast, phone getting hot when idle, data usage spiking unexpectedly — these are signs of malware running in the background.

✓ Step 5

Install a reputable antivirus app

Malwarebytes (free), Bitdefender, or Avast Mobile Security — all on Google Play Store — can scan for these specific malware families.

✓ Step 6 — If applicable

Bought a budget phone from an informal market?

Do a full factory reset, reinstall only verified Play Store apps, then install an antivirus before adding any banking apps.

What to Do If You Think You’re Already Infected

🚨 Act immediately — follow this order

Do these four steps in sequence

  • First: Log into your mobile banking app on WiFi and freeze or temporarily disable your account before doing anything else.
  • Then: Call your bank’s fraud line from a different phone and report a potential compromise. Ask about reversing suspicious transactions.
  • Then: Download Malwarebytes from Google Play Store and run a full device scan.
  • Finally: If the scan finds infections, do a factory reset — then reinstall apps one by one from the Play Store only.

✓ Report any confirmed malware infection to ngCERT at incident@cert.gov.ng or visit cert.gov.ng. Your report helps protect other Nigerians.

Source: ngCERT Official Advisory — cert.gov.ng  |  Technology Times Nigeria — technologytimes.ng
